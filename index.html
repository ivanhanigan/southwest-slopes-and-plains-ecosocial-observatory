1 Introduction

All societies can be conceptualised as eco-social systems in which social processes continually interact with numerous ecological factors (which themselves interact). All societies also create 'eco-social' observatories which survey and seek to influence the future.

Though the structure, formality and effectiveness of these observatories varies in essence functioning observatories generate policy options which can then be followed in ways which maximise (or at least preserve collective well-being).

However, if ecological constraints are too severe (such as the approaching limits of ecosystem services and life support systems, or an excessive energy cost), then consequences can include defective predictions and inappropriate (or ineffectual) policies. Political interference can also impair such observatories. Such failures of self-correction may generate positive feedbacks leading to tipping points beyond which human well-being declines, particularly if ecological constraints continue to worsen.
